The Cairn

Stack the stones you already gathered. Find where they balance, where the middle stone sits, and how far they scatter — then whether two piles rise and fall together.

This is data you already hold. Not a forecast — a finished pile of numbers you went out and collected. The Cairn describes it: its center, its spread, and how two columns move together. The split that matters for the whole course — the Cairn counts real stones and divides by n (or n−1 for a sample); the Weighing Table weighs a distribution of what could happen by probability. Same skeleton — center, spread, co-movement — different fuel.

Statistics app

From HOME open Statistics. Pick 1-Variable for one column, or 2-Variable (y=a+bx) for paired X and Y. Type each value, press = after each. If values repeat, switch the Frequency column on and enter counts.

Press OK / the on-screen Statistics result to read the summary.

1-Variable gives you

x̄ mean · Σx · Σx² · n · sx sample std (÷ n−1) · σx population std (÷ n) · minX, Q1, Median, Q3, maxX.

2-Variable adds

ȳ, sy, and the regression line a + bx with r — that r is exactly the correlation RXY.

The trap: sx is the sample one (n−1) the exam usually wants; σx is the population one (n). Same data, two buttons — read the one the question asks for.

Connected Spells

The Cairn describes a sample you already have. The Weighing Table does the same three moves — center, spread, co-movement — but on a probability distribution.

Tell them apart by the fuel: a list of observations (divide by n or n−1) is the Cairn; a column of P(x) that sums to 1 is the Weighing Table.

Formula Reference — Kap 1
X̄ — MeanCenter
X̄ = (1/n) · Σ xi
Add every value, divide by the count. The balance point — every stone pulls on it equally.
X̄v — Weighted MeanCenter
X̄v = Σ(xi · wi) / Σ wi
When values carry different importance. Heavier weights drag the center toward them.
MedianCenter
position = (n+1)/2   (average the two middle if n is even)
Sort first. The middle stone. Ignores how far the outliers reach — robust where the mean is dragged.
Quartiles & IQRCenter
Q1 pos = (n+1)/4   Q3 pos = 3(n+1)/4   IQR = Q3 − Q1
The quarter-marks. If a position lands between two stones, interpolate. IQR is the spread of the middle 50%.
ModeCenter
the most frequent value
The tallest stack. Modal percentage = (frequency of mode / n) · 100%.
Sx² — Sample VarianceSpread
Sx² = (1/(n−1)) · Σ(xi − X̄)²
Average squared distance from the mean. Divide by n−1 because the mean ate one degree of freedom.
S² — Population VarianceSpread
S² = (1/n) · Σ(xi − X̄)²
Same pile, divided by n. Use it when the data is the whole population, not a sample.
S ↔ SxSpread
S = √((n−1)/n) · Sx
Population std is always slightly smaller than sample std. The gap shrinks toward zero as n grows.
Sx — Standard DeviationSpread
Sx = √(Sx²)
Square root of variance — folds the pile back onto the original scale. If the data is in kroner, so is Sx.
SXY — Sample CovariancePairs
SXY = (1/(n−1)) · Σ(xi − X̄)(yi − Ȳ)
For each pair, multiply X’s distance from its mean by Y’s. Positive = move together, negative = move apart.
RXY — CorrelationPairs
RXY = SXY / (Sx · Sy)
Covariance stripped of units onto [−1, +1]. ±1 is a perfect line, 0 is no linear link.
