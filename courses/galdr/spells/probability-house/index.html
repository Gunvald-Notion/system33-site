Step 1 — Recognize the House

Two events in the problem — A and B. Each one either fires or doesn’t. Two forces × two states = four rooms.

When you read a problem and see two events discussed — “a student takes the bus (A), a student has homework (B)” — the house is already there. Your job is to see it.

The table above is the house. Four inner rooms. Row sums on the right. Column sums on the bottom. The corner is locked at 1 — always. That’s the conservation law made visible.

Step 2 — Build the Blueprint

Construct the house number-blind — no data yet. Five moves, each adding structure to the table above.

Name the two forces. Pick A and B from the problem. A runs down the rows. B runs across the columns. Each has a complement — ikke-A, ikke-B.

Add the row-sum column. Σ rad on the right. P(A) and P(ikke-A) live here — the total mass in each row, regardless of column.

Add the column-sum row. Σ kolonne at the bottom. P(B) and P(ikke-B) — mass in each column, regardless of row.

Lock the whole-house Σ at 1. Bottom-right corner. The total mass in the entire house. Always 1. The conservation law.

Name the four rooms. Each inner cell is an intersection. Top-left: A∩B. Top-right: A∩ikke-B. Bottom-left: ikke-A∩B. Bottom-right: ikke-A∩ikke-B. The full blueprint.

Step 3 — Drop the Data

Enter the values given in the problem. Place them carefully — only what the text tells you. Don’t compute anything yet.

Step 4 — Walk the Conservation Law

The house finishes itself. Every remaining cell is forced by the conservation law — rows sum to their margin, columns sum to their margin, everything sums to 1.
